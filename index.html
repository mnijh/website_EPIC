Introduction

Future space missions and ground based facilities will provide more accurate mass and radius estimates of exoplanets. In order to fully exploit the better observational data, accurate planetary interior structure models are needed from the theoretical side. We follow up on the work of Dorn et al. (2015) and Dorn et al. (2017) which presented a statistical method to characterize exoplanets. In this work we present an improved method to characterize exoplanets based on mass radius estimates. Including recently published equation of state, an improved treatment of water in the planetary atmosphere and the ability to model partially molten cores and mantles.

Internal Structure Model

The thermal and mechanical structure of a planet is the result of a variety of physical and chemical processes acting on various temporal and spatial scales. Given the necessity of running \(\sim 10^6\) models during the statistical inference per target, one has to simplify the model of our modelled planets. Hence the interior structure model describes a 1-D spherical symmetric sphere in hydrostatic equilibrium, which is made up of distinct layers of different composition (see Fig. 2).

Bayesian Inference

In order to characterize a planet we want to know which distribution of the model input parameters explains best some measured data of the planet. As in Dorn et al. (2015; 2017) we use a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method to perform the statistical inference. The basis is a simple Metropolis Hastings algorithm which for a point \(\textbf{m}_i\) in the space of model parameters proposes a new set of parameters \(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\) given a symmetric transition probability q(\(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\)|\(\textbf{m}_{i}\)). Next the prior p(\(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\)) and likelihood p(d|\(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\)) is evaluated at the proposed point. If the product of prior and likelihood is larger than for the current model \(\textbf{m}_{i}\), then \(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\) is accepted an added to the Markov chain. If the product is smaller, \(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\) can still be accepted with probability \begin{equation} P_\text{acc}=\frac{p(\textbf{m}_{i+1})p(\textbf{d}|\textbf{m}_{i+1})}{p(\textbf{m}_{i})p(\textbf{d}|\textbf{m}_{i})}. \end{equation} Otherwise \(\textbf{m}_{i+1}\) is rejected and \(\textbf{m}_i\) is added to the Markov chain. In order to speed up the algorithm we added some modifications compared to regular Metropolis Hastings MCMC.

  • We adaptively change the proposal distribution according to Haario et al. (2001).
  • For compositional parameters (i.e. parameters which lie on a simplex) we use the self-adjusting logit transform (SALT) proposal by Director et al. (2017) to propose the next move of the MCMC-chain.
  • Since some data variables (i.e. total mass, Si/Fe and Mg/Si of the planet) can be evaluated very fast, we pre-compute these parameters with a separate forward model. Then we test, if for an exact match of the remaining data variables, the model would be rejected. If true, then we skip the evaluation of the full forward model since it would not change the outcome of the acceptance process.
